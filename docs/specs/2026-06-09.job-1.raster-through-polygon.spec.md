## This job, in one line

Given a polygon (an Area of Interest) and a COG (or a stack of COGs across model
scenarios), compute a correct summary statistic — e.g. mean predicted cooling —
over only the pixels inside that polygon, on your own laptop.

## Inputs

- **An AOI** — a vector polygon (drawn by hand for now; the *automatic* drawing is
  Job 2's problem). Of arbitrary shape and size.
- **A COG / stack** — Cape Town's 63 COGs, or the Campinas COG from nb2. Public,
  no credentials (WRI Cities Indicators API + S3). Multiple model scenarios.
- **A CRS** — the coordinate reference system the data lives in.

> **Resolution / CRS caveat — read before quoting "1m."** Aman says the pixels are
> ~1m :78. But the repo computes resolution only once, in nb2, on a **Campinas**
> COG — and that COG's CRS is **geographic (degrees), not metric**. The "~1m" is a
> `cos(lat)` conversion from a pixel size of `9e-06°` (≈1.0m N-S, ≈0.92m E-W at
> −22.96°), so it changes with latitude. No one computed Cape Town's pixel size,
> and at −33.9° the E-W number differs. Two consequences: (a) don't state "Cape
> Town = 1m" as fact; (b) the data is in **degrees**, so a pixel is not a fixed
> ground area. Reproject to equal-area before any area calculation — mandatory, not
> optional (see challenge 3). Source: the "1 meter" caveat in
> `2026-06-06.Aman-Paul.on-Marimo-AI-pairing.reproduction-notes.md`.

## The task

1. Read **only the bytes under the AOI** from the COG via a windowed/range read —
   not the whole file.
2. **Rasterize the polygon** onto the pixel grid and decide what counts as
   "inside" at the boundary.
3. Compute a statistic (sum / mean / count) over the inside pixels.
4. Repeat across the **scenario stack**, with the layers pixel-aligned.
   (Computing the statistic per scenario is Job 1; *comparing* scenarios or
   intervention types is Job 3 — `2026-06-09.job-3.cross-objective-comparison.spec.md`.)

## The real challenges (where correctness leaks)

This is bounded, learnable work. The difficulty is getting it *exactly right*, not
whether it's possible.

1. **Windowed reads.** At this resolution a city runs to ~10⁸ pixels per band per
   scenario — too large to load. The whole point of a COG is to fetch only the
   window that intersects the AOI (rasterio/rioxarray windowed read; overviews for
   coarse passes). Get this wrong and you either download everything or read the
   wrong window.
2. **Polygon → pixel mask.** The AOI is a smooth shape; the data is a grid of
   squares. You must choose the boundary rule — pixel-centroid-inside vs.
   any-touch. Per-pixel error is tiny at 1m, but the rule matters the moment Job 2
   *compares* many polygons of different sizes.
3. **CRS / equal-area.** Not hypothetical for this data: the Campinas COG is in a
   **geographic (degree) CRS**, so a pixel is *not* a fixed ground area — its size
   changes with latitude. Any "return per area" comparison must reproject to an
   equal-area CRS first, or it silently biases toward certain latitudes/shapes.
   (And confirm Cape Town's CRS independently — don't assume it matches Campinas.)
4. **Stack alignment.** 63 COGs across scenarios, plus separate intervention
   layers, must share grid, resolution, and extent. Misalignment forces resampling
   → more error and cost.

## Tools

Python: `rasterio` / `rioxarray` (windowed reads, masking), `shapely` (geometry),
Marimo (the paired notebook). These are the standard, well-maintained libraries
for exactly this; no custom raster engine needed. Confirm versions before relying
on specific APIs.

## Definition of done

- Compute a correct per-AOI statistic on a **known, hand-drawn AOI** in Cape Town,
  reading only that AOI's window from the COG.
- **Cross-check** the number a second way (e.g. a full-raster mask at coarse
  resolution, or a hand calculation on a tiny test polygon) so it isn't just
  "it ran."
- Reproducible from a clean checkout on Paul's laptop (`uvx marimo edit`), data
  pulled live, no credentials.

## What you actually learn (be honest about this)

**Durable (keep these):**
- **Vector meets raster** — smooth polygon over a pixel grid; what's "inside,"
  where edge error lives. The foundational geospatial idea; transfers everywhere.
- **Projections lie** — why areas/distances are wrong in lat/lon, why you need
  equal-area before comparing anything.
- **Computing over data bigger than memory** — read only the window you need, work
  coarse-then-fine. Not even geo-specific; transfers to any tiled/columnar data.
- **A feel for the data** — what sub-block resolution permits, what's cheap vs. expensive.
  You can only get this by handling it, and it's the real prerequisite for having
  judgment on Job 2's scoring.

**What this does NOT teach:** anything about Job 2's hard part. Masking polygons
and fixing CRS teaches you nothing about defining "best" or making the search
tractable. Job 1 is the substrate, not a ramp into the optimization.

**The honest caveat:** the durable concepts above are a couple of focused days,
not a week. Most of the calendar time is forgettable API friction that teaches you
*this toolchain*, not geospatial thinking. Extract the four items above on
purpose; don't mistake a week of working code for a week of learning.

## Open questions

- What exactly is the per-pixel quantity being summed? "Cooling benefit" is a
  modeling choice, not a given — and it shades into Job 2's scoring problem.
- Which boundary rule (centroid vs. any-touch) does the downstream comparison need?
- Equal-area projection: which one, and is it already the COG's native CRS?

## Relationship to Job 2

Job 2 calls this calculation thousands of times over candidate areas. If Job 1's
statistic is additive over pixels, Job 2 can precompute it once (summed-area
table) and evaluate any rectangle in O(1). So **how you define the Job 1 statistic
directly determines whether Job 2 is fast or intractable.** Decide it with Job 2
in mind. See `2026-06-09.job-2.aoi-optimization-search.spec.md`.
