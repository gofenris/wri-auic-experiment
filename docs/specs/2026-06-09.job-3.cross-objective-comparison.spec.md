## Why this is its own job

Jobs 1 and 2 both assume **one** intervention and **one** per-pixel quantity. Job 1
scores a single AOI; Job 2 searches for the best AOI *for that one intervention*.
Neither owns the question Aman raised:

> "COGs for different objectives are not going to be commensurate, or shouldn't be
> assumed to be commensurate… Comparison of two different intervention types
> (planting trees vs painting roofs) will require some sort of heuristic scoring
> eventually." — Aman, 2026-06-09

So the moment you compare *different* interventions — or different scenarios for the
same area — the raw numbers don't line up. Different units, different costs,
different effects. Picking the larger number is wrong. Making them comparable needs
a scoring step that lives nowhere in Jobs 1 or 2.

Aman's guidance: keep this **separate**. Job 1 may mechanically compute the same
statistic across a scenario stack; *deciding which one wins* is this job.

## This job, in one line

Given per-objective statistics from Job 1 (one number per AOI per intervention or
scenario), produce a comparison that ranks them on a common scale.

## Aman's difficulty ladder (his framing, 2026-06-09)

1. **Same intervention, same-size areas** — directly comparable. Start here.
2. **Same intervention, different-size areas** — compensate by normalizing per area.
   Straightforward in principle (reuse Job 1's equal-area reprojection); Aman flags
   it "may not be straightforward practically."
3. **Different intervention types** (e.g. trees vs. roofs — Aman's examples) —
   requires a heuristic scoring function to put incommensurate outputs on one scale.
   This is the hard part and the reason this job exists.

## What this job does NOT decide

The scoring function itself is a modeling / product choice, not a technical default.
Leave it blank until Aman (and the client) specify it. Do not invent a weighting.
